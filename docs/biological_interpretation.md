# Biological Interpretation

## Overview

The maximum-likelihood phylogenetic tree broadly separated the curated tubulin sequences into three major groups corresponding to alpha-, beta-, and gamma-tubulins. This pattern suggests that the main tubulin types in the dataset represent distinct paralogous groups within the tubulin protein family.

## Alpha- and Beta-Tubulin Groups

The alpha- and beta-tubulin sequences formed separate groups in the tree. This is consistent with their known roles as related but distinct tubulin subfamilies. Alpha- and beta-tubulin interact to form the core heterodimeric building block of microtubules, but they are not identical proteins and have diverged as separate paralogous lineages.

Within the alpha- and beta-tubulin groups, some closely related organisms clustered together, such as human and mouse sequences. However, not every internal relationship was strongly resolved, so the deeper branching patterns within each tubulin type should be interpreted cautiously.

## Gamma-Tubulin Group

The gamma-tubulin sequences also formed a separate group. This supports the distinction between gamma-tubulin and the alpha/beta-tubulins. Gamma-tubulin has a different cellular role, mainly associated with microtubule nucleation rather than forming the main microtubule polymer with alpha- and beta-tubulin.

Some gamma-tubulin branches were relatively long, especially for more divergent organisms. This may reflect sequence divergence, limited taxon sampling, or differences in evolutionary rate among lineages.

## Notes on Dataset Limitations

The dataset was designed to be small and manually curated rather than exhaustive. One representative alpha-, beta-, and gamma-tubulin sequence was selected per organism where possible. Reviewed UniProt entries were preferred, but a few entries had lower protein evidence levels or limited exact-species availability.

The Trypanosoma entries were labelled as `Trypanosoma_brucei_complex` because exact-species reviewed entries were limited, and the selected alpha-, beta-, and gamma-tubulin entries were not all from the same subspecies. The gamma-tubulin entry for this group was unreviewed, so its placement should be interpreted with additional caution.

## Conclusion

Overall, the tree supports the expected separation of alpha-, beta-, and gamma-tubulins into distinct evolutionary groups. The analysis shows that the major tubulin types are more strongly grouped by protein type than by organism alone, consistent with the interpretation that these tubulin classes represent ancient paralogous subfamilies.
